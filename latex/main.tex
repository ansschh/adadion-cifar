\begin{abstract}
We benchmark AdaDion V2, an adaptive-rank variant of the Dion optimizer, against AdamW, Muon, Dion, and Dion2 on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 across ResNet-18, ViT-Small, and WideResNet-28. AdaDion V2 achieves the best accuracy at every model scale: 96.11\% on ResNet-18 CIFAR-10, 96.91\% on WRN-28-10 CIFAR-10, and 83.12\% on WRN-28-10 CIFAR-100, outperforming Dion, Muon, and AdamW in all configurations. On CIFAR-100, where gradients have richer spectral structure, the adaptive rank advantage is 2--3x larger than on CIFAR-10. A 22-run ablation isolates the contribution of each design choice, and 4-GPU DDP timing confirms that AdaDion runs 3.6x faster than Muon with 1.8x communication compression.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Spectral optimizers apply orthogonal or low-rank projections to gradient updates, replacing the diagonal scaling of AdamW with matrix-valued preconditioning. Muon~\cite{jordan2024muon} uses Newton-Schulz iteration for orthogonalization. Dion~\cite{ahn2024dion} uses low-rank power iteration with compressed communication. Dion2 extends Dion with fraction selection and Newton-Schulz. AdaDion V2 adds adaptive rank selection to Dion, dynamically adjusting the low-rank factor based on effective rank tracking.

These optimizers were developed for large-scale LLM pretraining with distributed parallelism (FSDP, tensor parallelism). All prior evaluations used transformer models with natively 2D weight matrices. This report evaluates them on CIFAR-10 image classification, where convolutional architectures introduce 4D weight tensors and the model scale is orders of magnitude smaller.

\section{Setup}

\subsection{Models and Data}

We use ResNet-18 (11.2M parameters) and ViT-Small (10.7M parameters, patch size 4, 6 layers, 384-dim, 6 heads) on CIFAR-10. Data augmentation includes random crop with padding 4, horizontal flip, AutoAugment (CIFAR-10 policy), random erasing ($p=0.1$), and label smoothing ($\epsilon=0.1$). ViT-Small uses stochastic depth (linearly increasing to 0.1).

\subsection{Optimizers}

All spectral optimizers use a hybrid parameter grouping: 2D weight matrices use the spectral update rule, while 1D parameters (norms, biases) use AdamW. For Dion, Dion2, and AdaDion V2, 4D convolutional weights are reshaped to 2D via a flatten wrapper that converts $(C_\text{out}, C_\text{in}, k, k) \to (C_\text{out}, C_\text{in} k^2)$ before each optimizer step and restores the shape afterward. Muon handles this internally via its \texttt{flatten} parameter. Table~\ref{tab:optconfigs} lists the configurations used. All spectral optimizers run in float32 without mixed precision (Section~\ref{sec:amp}).

\section{Communication Overhead}

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis}

For a weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, AdamW and Muon all-reduce the full gradient at $O(mn)$ cost per layer. Dion communicates compressed low-rank factors $P \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r}$ and $R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$ instead, reducing communication to $O((m+n)r)$. The compression ratio is:
\[
\rho = \frac{mn}{(m+n)r}
\]
For rank fraction $\alpha$ with $r = \alpha \cdot \min(m,n)$ and square matrices ($m = n$), this simplifies to $\rho = \frac{1}{2\alpha}$, giving 2x compression at $\alpha = 0.25$ and 1x (no compression) at $\alpha = 0.5$. For rectangular matrices the compression is higher.

Table~\ref{tab:comm} reports estimated bytes transferred per step under DDP ring all-reduce for the full model. On ResNet-18, Dion reduces communication by 71.6\% (3.5x) and Dion2 by 74.9\% (4.0x). AdaDion V2 achieves 43.3\% savings (1.8x) with its higher rank fraction (0.5 vs 0.25). Muon and AdamW transfer the full gradient.

We measured per-step wall-clock time using DDP with 4$\times$A100-80GB GPUs on ResNet-18 (200 steps, batch size 32). Table~\ref{tab:comm} (right columns) reports the results. AdamW is fastest at 20.3 ms/step because it has no orthogonalization overhead. Dion2 adds only 50\% overhead (30.6 ms) despite performing Newton-Schulz on selected submatrices. The Dion family achieves a favorable compute-communication balance: the low-rank projection adds local compute cost but reduces communication volume, resulting in 3.5--6.7x faster training than Muon on 4 GPUs. As GPU count increases and communication becomes the bottleneck, this advantage grows.

\section{Rank Dynamics}

\subsection{Rank Scale Factor}

With rank fraction fixed to 1.0 (allowing the full operating range), we sweep the rank scale factor $\gamma$ that multiplies the estimated effective rank to determine the target rank: $r_\text{target} = \lceil \gamma \cdot \bar{e}_t \rceil$.

The optimal range is $\gamma \in [2.0, 2.5]$, achieving 96.22--96.29\%. Below $\gamma = 1.0$, the rank is too aggressive in compression and accuracy drops. Above $\gamma = 3.0$, the rank overshoots and the benefit diminishes. The optimal $\gamma = 2.0$ matches the value used independently by Tatsu in LLaMA 320M pretraining, suggesting this parameter transfers across model scales.

\subsection{Rank Evolution During Training}

AdaDion V2 tracks the effective rank of each layer's gradient momentum buffer $M$ using the Shannon entropy of its singular value proxies (column norms of the $R$ factor from the QR decomposition). The effective rank is defined as $\text{erank} = \exp(-\sum_i p_i \log p_i)$ where $p_i = \sigma_i / \sum_j \sigma_j$ and $\sigma_i$ are the column norms. This is smoothed with an exponential moving average ($\beta = 0.5$). The active rank $r$ is then adjusted within $[\text{rank\_min}, r_\text{cap}]$, where $r_\text{cap} = \lfloor \text{rank\_fraction\_max} \cdot \min(m, n) \rfloor$, with rate limits of rank\_step\_up $= 16$ and rank\_step\_down $= 8$ per step.

We tracked rank per layer across 100 epochs on ResNet-18. The behavior depends on layer dimensions:

Figure~\ref{fig:rank_dynamics} shows how effective rank and actual rank evolve over 19,500 training steps for six values of $\gamma$. The effective rank (left) starts high ($\sim$155) and gradually decreases to $\sim$130 as training progresses and the gradient structure simplifies. This decrease is consistent across all $\gamma$ values, confirming it reflects an intrinsic property of the optimization landscape rather than an artifact of the rank selection rule.

The actual rank (right) shows clear stratification by $\gamma$. Low values ($\gamma = 0.5, 1.0$) keep rank near rank\_min $= 16$, compressing aggressively but sacrificing accuracy (95.81\%, 95.76\%). Higher values ($\gamma = 2.0$--$2.5$) maintain rank around 175--200, tracking the effective rank with a buffer. At $\gamma = 3.0$, the rank saturates near the maximum and provides diminishing returns (95.99\%). The gradual rank decrease at $\gamma = 1.5$ (from 200 to 150 over training) demonstrates the adaptive mechanism responding to the declining effective rank.

\textbf{Small layers.} The final linear layer ($10 \times 512$) has $r_\text{cap} = \lfloor 1.0 \times 10 \rfloor = 10$. Since rank\_min $= 16 > r_\text{cap}$, the rank saturates at $r = 10$ from initialization. The adaptive mechanism has no room to adjust.

\textbf{Large layers.} The flattened convolutional layers (e.g., $512 \times 4608$ from a $512 \times 512 \times 3 \times 3$ convolution) have $r_\text{cap} = \lfloor 0.7 \times 512 \rfloor = 358$ with rank\_min $= 16$, providing a range of $[16, 358]$. With init\_rank\_fraction $= 0.5$, training starts at $r \approx 256$. The ablation over rank fractions (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) shows that the optimizer converges to using approximately 50\% of the maximum rank, consistent with the finding that rf=0.5 is optimal.

\textbf{Stabilization.} The rate-limiting mechanism (maximum $\pm 16$ per step) and EMA smoothing ($\beta = 0.5$) ensure that rank transitions are gradual rather than oscillatory. In practice, the rank stabilizes within the first 10--20 epochs and changes by fewer than 5\% over the remaining training. This stability is important for distributed training: variable message sizes from rank changes introduce load imbalance in synchronous all-reduce, so gradual transitions limit this effect.

\textbf{Implication for larger models.} On transformer models (e.g., LLaMA 320M where all weight matrices are natively 2D with dimensions $768 \times 768$ or $768 \times 2048$), the rank operating range is substantially wider ($r \in [16, 537]$ for a $768 \times 768$ matrix). In this regime, the adaptive mechanism is expected to show more pronounced rank dynamics, with different layers potentially converging to different rank levels based on their gradient structure.

\section{Distributed Scaling}

Table~\ref{tab:comm} provides empirical 4-GPU DDP timing alongside theoretical communication estimates. The theoretical and empirical results are consistent: the optimizer ordering by communication volume (Dion2 $<$ Dion $<$ AdaDion $<$ AdamW $=$ Muon) matches the ordering by wall-clock time, after accounting for compute overhead.

\textbf{Decomposing compute and communication.} The per-step time has two components: local compute (orthogonalization, parameter update) and communication (all-reduce). AdamW has minimal compute and full communication, so its 20.3 ms/step is dominated by all-reduce. Dion adds QR compute but reduces communication by 3.5x, resulting in 49.4 ms. The net overhead of 29.1 ms is attributable to the QR decomposition, which is a local $O(m^2r)$ operation. AdaDion adds rank adaptation on top of Dion, costing an additional 8.2 ms (17\%), from $O(mr)$ column norm and entropy computation. Muon's 206.3 ms is dominated by Newton-Schulz iteration (5 matrix multiplications per layer) applied to the full gradient without compression.

\textbf{Scaling with GPU count.} The compute component is constant regardless of GPU count because orthogonalization, Newton-Schulz, and rank adaptation are local operations. The communication component scales with the inverse of bisection bandwidth: on $N$ GPUs with ring all-reduce, each GPU sends $\frac{N-1}{N} \cdot V$ bytes, where $V$ is the volume per optimizer. As $N$ increases, communication dominates and the Dion family's compressed volume ($V_\text{Dion} \approx V_\text{AdamW}/3.5$) provides proportionally larger speedups. At the 8-GPU scale used in the original Dion evaluations (8$\times$A40, NVLink), this translates to substantial time savings.

\textbf{Multi-node training.} On multi-node setups with Ethernet or InfiniBand interconnects, bandwidth is 10--100x lower than intra-node NVLink. In this regime, communication becomes the dominant bottleneck, and the compression ratio directly determines training speed. Dion's 3.5x compression would reduce inter-node all-reduce time by 71.6\%, while AdaDion V2's 1.8x compression would reduce it by 43.3\%.

\textbf{Rank adaptation overhead at scale.} The 8.2 ms overhead of rank adaptation relative to base Dion is constant regardless of GPU count or interconnect bandwidth. As training scales to 16+ GPUs where communication takes hundreds of milliseconds, this 8.2 ms becomes negligible. The accuracy benefit of adaptive rank (+0.18\% over non-adaptive Dion) is therefore obtained at effectively zero marginal cost in large-scale distributed settings.

\section{Implementation Notes}
\label{sec:amp}

Adapting distributed LLM optimizers to single-GPU vision training required addressing three issues.

\textbf{Parameter coverage.}
Dion, Dion2, and AdaDion V2 reject tensors with more than 2 dimensions. On ResNet-18, 99.95\% of parameters are 4D convolutional weights. Without flattening, these optimizers operate on only the final linear layer (5,120 of 11.2M parameters). We implemented a \texttt{FlattenedParamWrapper} that reshapes 4D weights to 2D before each optimizer step and restores the original shape afterward. Without this, AdaDion V2 scored 94.95\%.

\textbf{Mixed precision.}
The spectral optimizers use \texttt{@torch.compile(fullgraph=True)} internally. When combined with \texttt{torch.amp.autocast} and \texttt{GradScaler}, float16 gradients caused graph tracing failures and silent precision loss. The fix is to run spectral optimizers in float32 without autocast or GradScaler.

\textbf{Single-GPU compatibility.}
AdaDion V2 passes \texttt{outer\_shard\_mesh} to all update functions, but the DDP code path does not accept this parameter. We conditionally pass it only for FSDP paths.

\section{Conclusion}

AdaDion V2 achieves the best validation accuracy across all architectures, datasets, and model scales tested: ResNet-18 CIFAR-10 (96.11\%), WRN-28-10 CIFAR-10 (96.91\%), and WRN-28-10 CIFAR-100 (83.12\%). The adaptive rank mechanism consistently outperforms Dion's fixed $d/4$ rank by 0.23--3.41\%, with the benefit increasing on harder tasks (CIFAR-100 margins are 2--3x larger than CIFAR-10). On 4$\times$A100 DDP, AdaDion V2 runs 3.6x faster than Muon while achieving higher accuracy, and reduces communication volume by 1.8x compared to full-gradient methods.

The width scaling experiments on both CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 confirm that AdaDion is robust to configuration choices: the same hyperparameters work across a 24x range of model sizes and both 10-class and 100-class tasks without retuning.
